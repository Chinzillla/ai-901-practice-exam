# Module 1: Introduction to AI Concepts

## Unit 7: Responsible AI

### Source

Microsoft Learn: [Responsible AI](https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/training/modules/get-started-ai-fundamentals/7-responsible-ai)

### Unit Overview

This unit introduces responsible AI as the practice of building and operating AI systems with safeguards that reduce the risk of harmful, illegal, offensive, unfair, unsafe, or inappropriate outcomes. Learners explore key responsible AI principles and apply them to common AI scenarios.

### Learning Objectives

By the end of this unit, learners should be able to:

- Define responsible AI.
- Explain why responsible AI should be considered throughout the AI lifecycle.
- Identify key responsible AI principles.
- Recognize examples of fairness, reliability, privacy, inclusiveness, transparency, and accountability.
- Choose responsible AI considerations for scenario-based requirements.

### Key Terms

| Term | Definition |
| --- | --- |
| Responsible AI | Practices and principles for building AI systems that reduce harm and support trustworthy use. |
| Guardrail | A safeguard that helps reduce risk in an AI system. |
| Content filter | A mechanism that helps reduce harmful or inappropriate generated content. |
| Fairness | Treating people and groups equitably and avoiding unjust discrimination. |
| Reliability and safety | Ensuring AI systems behave dependably and avoid unsafe outcomes. |
| Privacy and security | Protecting personal data and preventing unauthorized access or misuse. |
| Inclusiveness | Designing AI systems that work for people with diverse needs. |
| Transparency | Helping users understand when and how AI is being used. |
| Accountability | Ensuring people and organizations remain responsible for AI system outcomes. |

### Lesson Flow

#### 1. Introduce Responsible AI

Explain that AI systems can affect people, processes, and decisions. Responsible AI focuses on reducing risks and making AI systems trustworthy.

#### 2. Guardrails and Content Filters

Describe guardrails as safeguards that help prevent harmful outcomes. Content filters are one example, but responsible AI requires more than filtering; it must be considered from planning through operation.

#### 3. Core Principles

Review the responsible AI principles:

- Fairness
- Reliability and safety
- Privacy and security
- Inclusiveness
- Transparency
- Accountability

#### 4. Apply the Principles

Use scenarios:

- Admissions systems should avoid unfair discrimination.
- Robots should avoid physical harm when confidence is low.
- Facial identification systems should protect personal images.
- Speech-based agents should provide captions for users with hearing impairments.
- Loan systems should disclose AI use and explain relevant data features.

#### 5. Responsible AI Across the Lifecycle

Emphasize that responsible AI is not a final checklist. It should shape design, implementation, testing, deployment, monitoring, and operation.

### Suggested Activities

#### Activity 1: Match the Principle

Give learners scenarios and ask them to match each one to a responsible AI principle.

#### Activity 2: Risk Brainstorm

Ask learners to identify risks for a simple AI assistant, then propose guardrails.

#### Activity 3: Improve the Scenario

Present an AI system with a missing responsible AI consideration and ask learners to improve it.

### Checks for Understanding

1. What is responsible AI?
2. Why are content filters not enough by themselves?
3. What are the key responsible AI principles?
4. Which principle is involved when a system discloses AI use?
5. Which principle is involved when personal images are deleted after use?
